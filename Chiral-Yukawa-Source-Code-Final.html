Chiral–Yukawa Force

\( \vec{F} = F_r \hat{r} \;+\; F_c \hat{t} \)

\( F_r = -\dfrac{k m_1 m_2}{r^2} \,\Big[1 - \beta e^{-(r/r_0)^2}\Big] \)
\( F_c = \dfrac{\sigma m_1 m_2}{r^2}\cdot\big(1-e^{-r^2/\epsilon^2}\big) \)
What does this mean?

• At large distances, the force is attractive, like gravity.
• At short distances, the Gaussian notch makes the radial part repulsive (prevents collision).
• The tangential chiral term (\(σ\)) twists orbits, injecting/removing angular momentum; the taper multiplier ensures the tangential part stays finite as \(r\to0\).
This force is non-conservative, so energy isn’t fixed.

Why isn’t energy constant?

In physics, energy is conserved only for conservative forces (derived from a potential).

The radial force is mostly conservative — like gravity, with a repulsive Gaussian core.

But the chiral tangential force (\(F_c\)) is not conservative: it applies sideways pushes that either speed up or slow down particles.

This means it is constantly doing work, adding/removing energy and angular momentum.

The simulation is designed this way intentionally, to explore non-conservative exotic dynamics.
